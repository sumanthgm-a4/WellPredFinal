Stroke Awareness: Recognizing, Preventing, and Managing Stroke

A stroke happens when blood flow to a part of the brain is cut off, depriving brain cells of oxygen and nutrients. This can cause severe damage and requires immediate medical attention.

  • Ischemic Stroke – Caused by blocked arteries (most common).
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke – Caused by a blood vessel rupture.
  • Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA) – A "mini-stroke" with temporary symptoms.

  • F – Face Drooping: One side of the face may droop.
  • A – Arm Weakness: Sudden weakness or numbness in one arm.
  • S – Speech Difficulty: Slurred or strange speech.
  • T – Time to Call Emergency Services: Call for help immediately!

While some risk factors can’t be changed, lifestyle modifications can significantly reduce stroke risk:

  • Manage Blood Pressure: Reduce salt intake and exercise regularly.
  • Control Diabetes: Monitor glucose levels and follow a healthy diet.
  • Quit Smoking & Limit Alcohol: Both increase stroke risk.
  • Stay Physically Active: Aim for 30 minutes of exercise 5 times a week.
  • Maintain a Healthy Diet: Focus on whole grains, lean proteins, and fruits & vegetables.

  • Eat More: Fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, nuts, and seeds.
  • Avoid: Processed foods, excessive salt, sugary drinks, and trans fats.
  • Drink plenty of water and limit sugary drinks.

Recovery varies per person, but rehabilitation may include:

  • Physical therapy to regain movement.
  • Speech therapy for communication issues.
  • Support groups for emotional well-being.
If you or a loved one experiences stroke symptoms, seek emergency help immediately!
